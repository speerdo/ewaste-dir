# How to Sell Your Old Electronics Directly and Get More Than Trade-In

Retailer trade-in programs are convenient. They are also, in most cases, the lowest-paying option available to you. [Amazon](https://amzn.to/3Kk2E45), Apple, and Best Buy build a margin into every quote they offer, which means you are leaving 20 to 40 percent of your device's value on the table in exchange for the convenience of a prepaid shipping label.

For a lot of people that tradeoff is worth it. If you want a faster, easier path, [our post](/blog/trade-in-vs-recycling) on [Amazon Trade-In](https://amzn.to/3Kk2E45) vs. Recycling covers how those programs compare. But if you want to know what your device could actually fetch on the open market, and which platforms make direct selling genuinely manageable, this guide is for you.

The difference between a trade-in and a private sale on the right platform is often $100 to $200 on a single device. For a household clearing out a drawer of old phones and a laptop, that gap adds up quickly.

## The Devices Most Worth Checking First

Not everything deserves a trip to eBay. Old VCRs, ancient desktop towers, and generic Android phones from 2017 probably have no resale market worth your time. But the following categories regularly hold value longer than people expect.

**iPhones**

Apple devices hold resale value better than almost anything else in consumer electronics. An iPhone 13 released in 2021 still sells for $150 to $250 in good condition in 2026. An iPhone 15 that is only a year old can fetch $400 or more. If you upgraded recently and your old phone is sitting in a drawer, it is almost certainly worth more than $0.

The condition matters a lot. A cracked screen drops the value significantly. A phone that still powers on and has no major damage can usually find a buyer.

**MacBooks and MacBook Pros**

Apple laptops from 2019 onward, especially the M1 and M2 chip models released starting in 2020, hold value unusually well. An M1 MacBook Air in good condition sells for $500 to $700 on the used market. Even a 2019 Intel MacBook Pro can bring $300 to $400.

**iPads**

Tablets generally lose value faster than phones, but iPad Pros and recent iPad Airs are exceptions. A 2021 iPad Pro 12.9 inch still commands $400 to $600 depending on storage size and condition. Standard iPads from 2020 or later typically sell for $100 to $200.

**Samsung Galaxy Flagships**

Samsung S-series phones from the last three years have a healthy used market. An S23 or S24 can bring $200 to $400 depending on storage and condition. Mid-range Samsung models hold much less value and are usually better recycled.

**Gaming Consoles**

PlayStation 5 consoles are still selling for close to retail on the used market given ongoing supply constraints in some configurations. Nintendo Switch OLED models hold value well. Xbox Series X units sell for $250 to $350 used. If you have a console sitting unused, check current prices before dropping it at a collection event.

**AirPods and Premium Wireless Headphones**

AirPods Pro (second generation) sell for $100 to $150 used. Sony WH-1000XM5 and similar premium noise-canceling headphones hold $150 to $250 in resale value. These are small and easy to ship, which makes selling them much less hassle than a laptop.

## Where to Sell Directly

**Swappa (Best for Phones and Tablets)**

Swappa is a peer-to-peer marketplace built specifically for phones, tablets, laptops, and gaming gear. Every listing is reviewed before going live, which keeps the platform cleaner than general marketplaces. Fees are paid by the buyer rather than the seller, which means you keep more of the final price.

For a working iPhone or Samsung flagship, Swappa typically returns 30 to 50 percent more than a retailer trade-in. You set your price, list the device, and usually find a buyer within a few days to two weeks. Payment is processed through PayPal, so you have purchase protection on both sides.

**eBay (Widest Reach, More Work)**

eBay reaches the largest pool of potential buyers for most electronics categories. This matters especially for older or niche devices that trade-in programs will not accept or value at zero. CRT TVs with gaming appeal, vintage iPods, older MacBook models, discontinued consoles: these have dedicated buyers on eBay who search specifically for them and pay more than you would expect.

The process requires more effort. You need clear photos, an accurate description, appropriate packaging, and a trip to the post office. Fees run around 12 to 15 percent of the sale price. For a $50 item the effort may not be worth it. For a $300 item the extra $80 to $100 over a trade-in is usually worth an hour of work.

One specific advantage eBay has over every other platform: sold listings. Before you list anything anywhere, search eBay for your exact device and filter results to show completed sales rather than active listings. Completed sales show what buyers actually paid, not what sellers hoped to get. This is the most reliable price signal available and takes about two minutes.

**Facebook Marketplace (Best for Local Sales)**

For larger items like monitors, desktop computers, gaming consoles, and laptops, Facebook Marketplace is worth trying before anything that requires shipping. You avoid packaging and post office trips entirely, get cash the same day, and buyers can inspect the device in person before handing over money.

The main friction points are lowball offers and buyers who do not show up. Price based on eBay sold listings rather than gut feel, write a specific description that includes model, storage size, and any cosmetic issues, and you will typically move anything with real demand within a week. Meeting at a public place like a coffee shop or bank lobby is the standard practice for safety.

**Back Market (For Refurbished-Quality Devices)**

Back Market is a marketplace for refurbished electronics where professional refurbishers buy and sell devices. If your phone or laptop is in genuinely good condition, you can list it directly for sale to the Back Market buyer pool. Prices trend slightly below eBay but the process is more structured and the buyers are vetted.

## How to Research Your Device's Value in Five Minutes

The eBay sold listings method works for almost any device. Search for your exact model, include the storage size if relevant (such as "iPhone 15 256GB"), and filter to show completed listings. Sort by most recent. The price range across the last dozen sales is your realistic selling range on the open market.

For phones specifically, SellCell aggregates current buyback offers from multiple trade-in buyers in a single search. This is useful as a baseline even if you plan to sell directly, because it tells you the floor price: what you could get with zero effort. If a direct sale on Swappa would net you $100 more, that gap tells you whether the extra work is worth it.

For laptops, cross-reference eBay sold listings with Back Market's current inventory for your model. If refurbishers are selling your model for $600, they are probably paying $350 to $450 to acquire it. That gives you context for pricing your listing competitively.

## What Actually Affects Resale Value

**Condition** matters more than anything else. A cracked screen cuts value by 40 to 60 percent on most devices. A device that does not power on is worth almost nothing on the open market, though some trade-in programs will still take it.

**Storage size** matters for phones and tablets. A 256GB iPhone will sell for $50 to $80 more than the same model in 64GB. If you bought a higher storage tier, that is reflected in resale value.

**Original accessories** help but are not required. The original box adds maybe $10 to $20 for most devices. Most buyers do not care about the original charger since cables are cheap and universal.

**Carrier lock status** matters for phones. An unlocked phone sells for more than a carrier-locked one because it works with any network. If your phone is paid off, contact your carrier to unlock it before selling. This is usually free and takes a few minutes.

**Age thresholds** are worth knowing. For iPhones, the value cliff tends to come around five to six years after release, when the device can no longer run the current iOS and app developers stop supporting it. For Android flagships, the drop happens faster, usually around three to four years. Beyond those thresholds, recycling is usually the right call.

## Before You List: Remove Your Data First

This step is not optional regardless of where the device is going. The specific steps depend on your device type, but the key principle is that a standard factory reset is sufficient for phones and tablets, while traditional spinning hard drives in older laptops require a dedicated overwrite tool like DBAN rather than just a reset. SSDs, which are standard in most laptops made after 2015, are adequately wiped by the built-in reset process.

For phones: sign out of your accounts first, then run a factory reset through the device settings. For iPhones, use Settings, General, Transfer or Reset, Erase All Content and Settings. For Android, go to Settings, General Management or System, then Factory Data Reset.

For laptops: sign out of all cloud services before wiping. Windows laptops use Settings, System, Recovery, Reset this PC. Mac laptops use System Settings, General, Transfer or Reset, Erase All Content and Settings.

One practical note specific to selling directly: buyers on Swappa and eBay will check that the device is not still linked to an Apple ID or Google account before they pay, since a device with activation lock cannot be set up by a new owner. Make sure to remove the device from your account during the reset process, not just perform the wipe.

## When to Skip Selling and Just Recycle

Not everything has a buyer worth pursuing. If the device has a cracked screen and repair costs exceed the resale value, if the battery will not hold a charge and replacement is not practical, if the model is too old to run current software, or if eBay sold listings show prices under $30, recycling is the right answer.

The time it takes to photograph, list, package, and ship a $25 item is rarely worth it. Drop it at a certified recycler and move on. The materials inside still have value even when the resale market for the whole device does not.

If it is worth less than $50 or it does not work, recycle it.
